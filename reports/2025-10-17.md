# EUR-Lex Daily Digest — 2025-10-17

## Executive Summary

### Key Items
- [1] The new Regulation (EU) 2025/1958 updates reporting requirements for supervisory financial information under Regulation (EU) 2015/534.
- [2] The Council Decision (EU) 2025/2126 authorizes the EU to sign an Amending Protocol with San Marino regarding automatic financial account information exchange.
- [3] The Council Decision (EU) 2025/2124 amends the existing agreement on automatic exchange of financial account information between the EU and Liechtenstein.
- [4] The Council Decision (EU) 2025/2120 authorizes the EU to sign an Amending Protocol with Andorra regarding automatic financial account information exchange.
- [5] The Council Decision (EU) 2025/2125 authorizes the EU to sign an Amending Protocol with Monaco regarding financial account information exchange.

### Briefing (~200 words)

The items [1] to [5] reflect significant regulatory developments within the European Union aimed at enhancing financial reporting and international tax compliance. 

Item [1] introduces Regulation (EU) 2025/1958, which updates supervisory financial reporting requirements, expanding the scope to include more financial institutions and mandating more frequent and detailed submissions. This regulation aims to improve compliance with supervisory standards by requiring institutions to adapt their reporting processes by January 1, 2026.

Items [2] to [5] involve Council Decisions authorizing the EU to sign Amending Protocols with San Marino, Liechtenstein, Andorra, and Monaco. These protocols focus on the automatic exchange of financial account information to bolster international tax compliance. Each protocol enhances existing agreements by expanding the scope of data shared and imposing stricter reporting obligations on financial institutions. The implementation timelines for these protocols are set for 2026, aligning with international standards.

Overall, the key themes across these items include increased regulatory oversight, enhanced data sharing practices, and a commitment to international tax compliance. The implications suggest that financial institutions will face heightened scrutiny and must adapt to more rigorous reporting requirements, while member states are expected to improve cooperation in tax matters.
