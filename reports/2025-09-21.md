**[1] [The effects of monetary policy on banks and non-banks in times of stress](https://www.ecb.europa.eu//pub/pdf/scpwps/ecb.wp3114~53f9ad5281.en.pdf)**

- The study examines the impact of monetary policy on banks and non-bank financial institutions (NBFIs), emphasizing financial stress effects.
- It identifies that monetary tightening results in significant reductions in total assets and debt holdings, especially for banks and investment funds.
- Loan responses vary, with money market and pension funds showing marked declines in loan exposures under high-stress conditions.
- Financial stress exacerbates the contractionary effects of monetary policy across all financial sectors and asset classes.
- The findings underscore the distinct vulnerabilities of financial intermediaries in the monetary policy transmission process.

**[2] [Stylized facts in money markets: an empirical analysis of the eurozone data](https://www.ecb.europa.eu//pub/pdf/scpwps/ecb.wp3113~9a3d30eabd.en.pdf)**

- The study analyzes interbank market transactions among the 47 largest eurozone banks using ECB's Money Markets Statistical Reporting database.

- The interbank network shows high stability, with increased density and symmetry in in- and out-degrees compared to unsecured markets.
- The implications affect major eurozone banks and regulatory bodies monitoring liquidity and market stability.

- It analyzes data from 18 advanced economies over 151 years, highlighting the broader impacts of inflation beyond monetary policy reactions.  
- A 1 percentage point rise in inflation is shown to double the probability of a financial crisis, indicating a strong causal relationship.  
- The research identifies a redistribution channel where inflation directly reduces real incomes, contributing to financial instability.  
- Findings suggest central banks face a complex trade-off when managing inflation, particularly in the context of tightening monetary policy.
